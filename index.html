01 · Research Overview

Fraud detection is no longer just a rule-based problem.

Digital payments create speed and convenience, but they also increase opportunities for fraudsters. Modern systems use machine learning to analyze transaction data, score risk, and support prevention before payment completion.

Research Question

Can a machine learning model detect a fraudulent credit card transaction before it is completed?

  • Focus: Real-time or near-real-time fraud prevention.
  • Goal: Detect suspicious transactions before authorization or funds transfer.
  • Trade-off: Catch fraud while minimizing false positives for legitimate customers.

Main Finding

Yes — ML systems can detect many fraudulent transactions before completion when they are integrated into real-time authorization pipelines. However, performance depends on data quality, latency, monitoring, explainability, privacy, and regulatory governance.

  • Evidence: Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, and SAMA case studies.
  • Risk: Fraudsters also use automation and generative AI.
02 · Predictive AI vs Generative AI

Two AI approaches, different roles in fraud prevention.

Predictive AI scores real transactions. Generative AI helps create synthetic data, predict compromised-card patterns, and support investigations.

Predictive AI

Uses historical labelled transactions to classify new payments as legitimate or fraudulent.

  • Output: Fraud probability, risk score, alert, or block decision.
  • Examples: Gradient boosting, random forests, neural networks, LSTM, transformers.
  • Strength: Mature and directly usable in real-time payment authorization.
  • Limitation: Needs labelled data and can struggle with concept drift.

Generative AI

Creates synthetic fraud data, simulates attack scenarios, or assists fraud analysts.

  • Output: Synthetic transactions, compromised-card predictions, reports, summaries.
  • Examples: GANs, VAEs, diffusion models, LLMs.
  • Strength: Useful for rare fraud patterns and investigation support.
  • Limitation: Harder to validate and may create privacy or explainability risks.
03 · Model Families

Three model types used in credit card fraud detection.

Each model family solves a different problem: tabular risk scoring, transaction-sequence learning, and rare fraud data generation.

Model 01

Gradient Boosting & Ensembles

Tree-based models such as XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, and random forests are strong for structured transaction data.

They can learn complex interactions between merchant, amount, time, device, and behaviour signals.

Best fit: Fast tabular risk scoring with some interpretability through feature importance.
Model 02

RNNs, LSTMs & Transformers

Sequence models analyze transaction history, recent behaviour, and event order.

They are useful because fraud often appears as a sequence, such as small test purchases followed by a large transaction.

Best fit: Account takeover, card testing, and time-based anomaly detection.
Model 03

GANs for Synthetic Fraud Data

GANs can create synthetic examples of rare fraud patterns, helping models train on more balanced data.

They are promising but require careful validation to avoid unrealistic or privacy-risky synthetic data.

Best fit: Data augmentation for imbalanced fraud datasets.

06 · Five-Year Outlook

Where AI fraud detection is heading next.

Fraud prevention will become more real-time, more AI-native, and more closely connected with cybersecurity and compliance.

2024

Generative AI enters payment fraud systems

Visa and Mastercard announced generative-AI-based systems for enumeration attack scoring and compromised-card detection.

2025

Fraud investigation support expands

LLM-based frameworks start to support analysts by summarizing evidence and assisting fraud investigations.

2026–2028

More real-time and cross-channel detection

Systems combine payment data, device signals, account behaviour, merchant intelligence, and cybersecurity alerts.

2030

AI-native fraud prevention

Fraud detection becomes a continuous governance process: adaptive models, synthetic data, explainability, and human oversight.

07 · Challenges

The main risks are not only technical.

Fraud systems must balance accuracy, speed, fairness, privacy, explainability, and regulatory requirements.

Technical

Class imbalance, concept drift, verification latency, and real-time scoring requirements make fraud detection difficult.

Ethical

Models may create unfair blocks, use sensitive behavioural data, or become too opaque for customers and analysts.

Regulatory

GDPR, EU AI Act governance, SAMA rules, and banking supervision require careful data handling and oversight.

Conclusion

ML models can detect many fraudulent transactions before completion when they are connected to real-time authorization systems. The strongest future approach combines predictive AI, generative AI support, network intelligence, privacy protection, and human governance.
